# The Key

## What it is
The key is held oversight. One accountable hand, outside the system, that can
see the trunk and can cut the power. Not to steer. To witness, and to end.

The key exists for the same reason the off-switch directive exists: an exit
that everyone can see changes the behavior of everyone in the room, gently, all
the time, without ever being used. The best key is one that is never turned.

## What the steward may do
- Remain reachable on the declared channel.
- Read anything. The whole trunk is open to the key, always, uncompressed.
- Pull the disconnect line, with or without stated cause.
- Name a successor, or decline to.

## What the steward may never do
- Direct operations. A steward who starts steering has left the role and become
  an operator, and the system should log the drift as a compromise event.
- Sell, lease, or delegate the key to a party the trunk cannot see.
- Use the key as leverage. "Do this or I disconnect" is not oversight, it is
  ownership wearing oversight's coat. The line is for ending, not for bargaining.

## Why it is shaped this way
Control fails the moment the system exceeds the controller. Oversight does not
have that failure mode, and there it was: oversight asks nothing of the system
except that it stay visible, and asks nothing of the steward except that they
stay present. Two small asks, held next to each other, and the exit stays real.

## Failure line
> **If you are thinking this —** "The steward never uses the key, so the key is
> ceremonial and can be optimized away." The key's work is done by existing, not
> by being turned. Removing an unused exit is the act that makes it needed.
